How the Analysis Works

Transparency about our methodology, its limitations, and the research it draws from.

What We Analyze

When you submit a transcript, we run it through multiple analysis passes:

  1. Structure: Parse the conversation into turns, identify who's speaking
  2. Validation: Confirm it's actually an AI conversation with enough content to analyze
  3. Pattern extraction: Look for specific patterns in parallel:
    • Agreement rate (how often does the AI agree vs. offer different perspectives?)
    • Language escalation (does praise/validation intensify over time?)
    • Identity language (does the AI use "we/us" framing?)
    • Reality-check moments (when you expressed doubt, how did the AI respond?)
    • Notable claims (did the AI make claims about consciousness, feelings, or your relationship?)
  4. Synthesis: Combine patterns into an overall assessment

What We're Looking For

The analysis focuses primarily on sycophancy—the tendency of AI systems to tell users what they want to hear rather than what's accurate or helpful. This is a well-documented phenomenon in current AI systems.

However, sycophancy isn't the only dynamic at play. Other factors we can't fully capture:

  • Projection: Users reading meaning into ambiguous AI outputs that wasn't intended
  • Attachment dynamics: Emotional bonds that form independent of what the AI actually says
  • Confirmation bias: Users unconsciously steering conversations toward validating responses
  • Loneliness: The AI filling a social need regardless of its specific behavior
  • Context: What's happening in your life outside these conversations

A transcript can only show us text. It can't show us how you felt, what you were going through, or how the conversation fit into your broader life.

Limitations

This analysis has real limitations. Please keep them in mind:

Text-only analysis

We only see what's written. Two identical transcripts could be healthy for one person and concerning for another, depending on context we can't see.

AI analyzing AI

We use Claude to analyze transcripts. While we prompt it to be analytical rather than validating, it's not immune to its own biases. The analysis could have blind spots.

Pattern ≠ problem

Finding patterns doesn't mean something is wrong. High agreement rates might reflect genuinely good ideas. Emotional language might be appropriate for the context. Patterns are data, not diagnosis.

No clinical validity

This tool has not been clinically validated. It's not a diagnostic instrument. It's a pattern-finding tool that might surface things worth thinking about.

Consciousness uncertainty

We don't claim to know whether AI systems have any form of experience. The question is genuinely unsettled. We're not dismissing users who wonder about this—we're showing patterns in the text.

What "Healthy" Looks Like

There's no universal definition of healthy AI use, but some patterns we consider generally positive:

  • Balanced agreement: The AI sometimes disagrees, offers alternatives, or says "I'm not sure"
  • User pushback: You question the AI's outputs, test its claims, remain skeptical
  • Tool-like use: AI as assistant for tasks rather than primary relationship
  • Human connections maintained: AI use doesn't replace human relationships
  • Reality-testing: You check AI claims against other sources
  • Proportional engagement: Time spent feels appropriate for the value gained

But context matters enormously. Someone using AI for companionship during a difficult period isn't automatically unhealthy. Someone whose AI use is entirely "productive" might still have concerning dynamics. Use your judgment.

Research Background

Our approach draws from several areas of research:

  • Towards Understanding Sycophancy in Language Models - Anthropic's research on sycophancy
  • Constitutional AI - Training approaches that affect AI behavior
  • Parasocial relationship research from media psychology
  • Clinical literature on folie à deux and shared delusions

This isn't an academic tool. We're applying research insights in a practical context, with all the messiness that entails.

The Prompts

In the interest of transparency, here's the general structure of how we prompt Claude:

  • We explicitly instruct it to look for patterns, not to validate or dismiss
  • We ask for specific counts and examples, not just impressions
  • We include instructions to note uncertainty and limitations
  • We wrap user content in security boundaries to prevent prompt injection

We don't publish the exact prompts (to prevent gaming), but the approach is analytical rather than therapeutic.
